## The Modern Flaw: How Regular Ghee is Made Today

To understand the value of the Bilona method, we first need to look at how 99% of commercial ghee is manufactured. The standard industrial process is entirely focused on speed and yield. It goes like this:

1. Massive quantities of milk (usually A1 milk from factory-farmed cows) are collected.
2. The milk is run through a centrifugal machine to instantly separate the cream.
3. This uncultured raw cream is then heated directly at extremely high temperatures until the moisture burns off, leaving behind a fat residue.

This process takes a few hours. But by skipping the culturing process and using direct high heat on raw cream, the molecular structure of the fat is altered. The resulting "ghee" is high in cholesterol-forming fats, lacks the complex aromatics of traditional ghee, and is practically devoid of the digestive enzymes and vitamins that make real ghee a superfood.

## The Bilona Method: The Ancient Art of True Ghee

The Bilona method is entirely different. It is a slow, methodical, deeply traditional process that respects the ingredient at every single step. It is the only way ghee is produced at Amrit Milk.

Here is how authentic A2 Bilona Ghee is crafted:

### Step 1: Boiling the A2 Milk

It all starts with pure, organic A2 milk from indigenous cows (like the Gir and Sahiwal breeds). The milk is gently boiled and then allowed to naturally cool to room temperature. This isn't just for safety; it begins the structural breakdown of the proteins.

### Step 2: Setting the Curd (Culturing)

Instead of skimming the cream off the milk, the entire batch of milk is inoculated with a natural starter culture and left overnight to ferment into curd (yogurt). This single step changes everything. The fermentation process introduces beneficial probiotics and completely alters the fat profile, making it significantly easier for human digestion.

### Step 3: The Bilona Churning

Before dawn, the curd is placed in an earthen or wooden vessel. A wooden churner—called a Bilona—is used to churn the curd bidirectionally (clockwise and counter-clockwise). This slow, mechanical churning separates the pure, cultured butter (Makkhan) from the buttermilk. Commercial machines break the fat molecules, but the traditional Bilona churning keeps them intact.

### Step 4: Slow Heating

Finally, the pure cultured butter is slowly heated in a thick-bottomed vessel over a low, steady flame. The moisture evaporates, and the milk solids separate and brown, leaving behind pure, liquid gold: A2 Bilona Ghee.
